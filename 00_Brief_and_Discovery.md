# Brief and Discovery: NetWorth Digital

## Who they are

NetWorth Digital is a digital agency that resells and implements GoHighLevel for Australian service-based SMBs. They package the GHL stack (CRM, marketing automation, websites, booking, follow-ups) as a productised service. Three core offers: strategy and optimisation calls, operations and automation builds, and websites and content.

+61 404 837 649

## Current site

A GoHighLevel funnel-template build (visible from the CSS naming: `TextElement`, `FormComponent`, `TestimonialModal`, etc). Hosted on `networth-digital.com`. The copy is loud, profane and hustle-coded ("WHY THE F*CK NOT", "BUSINESS IS F*CKING HARD"). It gets attention but it filters out the higher-paying clients who buy from calm authority, not noise.

## The strategic problem

The market they want is bigger budget service-based business owners. Detailers, trades, gyms, consultancies, agencies. Those buyers compare NetWorth Digital against other GHL implementers and against bigger marketing agencies. The current site does not pass the smell test in that comparison. It looks templated and reads as desperate.

The rebuild needs to do three things at once:

1. Reposition them from "loud GHL reseller" to "premium operations partner that happens to run on GHL".
2. Compress the buyer journey to a single, obvious next action (book a strategy call).
3. Show that they actually build the kind of systems they sell. The site itself is proof of capability.

## Target audience

Primary: Australian service-based business owners doing $250k to $2M revenue, three to fifteen staff, owner is still operator, drowning in admin, leads slipping through cracks. They are skeptical of marketing agencies. They want to see structure.

Secondary: Coaches and consultants who want a productised offer and need the systems behind it.

## What the new site has to do

Map of jobs:

- Reframe NetWorth Digital as authority, not hustle.
- Make the three offers (Strategy, Operations, Web) legible in under thirty seconds.
- Show before and after with a vs-comparison block (this is already a strength in the current copy, just needs better presentation).
- Hold the user's attention through scroll-driven storytelling (the work the user does scrolling becomes the demo).
- Drive every section to one CTA: "Book a strategy call".
- Look like the visitor's competitor already hired them.

## Inspiration analysed

Four references were uploaded. Pulled the relevant cues from each:

- **demo1 (Rig)**: warm off-white background, near-black, electric red accent. Numbered problem cards (001, 002, 003). Marquee strip of capability tags. Diagrammatic before/after illustration showing the offer visually. Confident, terse copy.
- **demo2 (Heimdall Power)**: heavy use of real product photography overlaid with floating data callouts. Case-study driven. Generous whitespace.
- **demo3 (Shift5)**: dark base, warm cream, orange accent. Letter-spaced display type for section headers. Floating telemetry-style data overlays that animate in. Editorial serif used sparingly for emphasis.
- **source (current NetWorth Digital)**: the only thing worth keeping from the current build is the vs-comparison section concept. Everything else gets replaced.

The palette and type direction lifts from demo1 and demo3. The "systems diagram as storytelling" lifts from demo1. The "data overlay floating on imagery" lifts from demo3. None of it gets copied directly. We are stealing the posture, not the pixels.

## Non-goals

- Not building a CMS. Copy lives in the codebase. Faster to iterate, no Webflow or WordPress.
- Not building a blog at launch. Phase two.
- Not building case study detail pages yet. One results section on the homepage, real case studies added once we have permission to publish.
- Not adding the live chat widget. Pushes everyone to book the call instead.

## Open questions for the client

1. Real testimonials and case study screenshots we can publish?
2. Logos of past clients we can show?
3. Founder photos and a one-paragraph bio for the about section?
4. Exact GHL features they want to highlight (or do we sell the outcome and hide the tool)?
5. Are they comfortable dropping the F-bombs in favour of confident, clean copy?
